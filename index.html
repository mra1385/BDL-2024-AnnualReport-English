*Section One: The General Economic Situation**

Although the risks of economic and financial fragmentation in the global economic landscape during 2024 have become somewhat more balanced, they still lean towards the downside, representing one of the most prominent threats to global stability. The escalation of geopolitical tensions, such as shifting alliances and conflicts, particularly in Ukraine and the Middle East, has increased the risks of disrupting trade, basic commodity markets, and reshaping investment flows, which has heightened uncertainty in trade policies and markets and harmed economic activity. Financial fragmentation is also deepening with the retreat of cross-border capital flows and increasing divergence in interest rates. Low-income countries are particularly vulnerable, facing difficulties in accessing financing and rising sovereign debt risks.

Indicators of stability began to emerge during most of 2024, following a long and difficult period of unprecedented shocks. Inflation rates have retreated from their highest levels in decades, showing a gradual decline despite some fluctuations, nearing central bank targets. Labor markets have also returned to their natural state, with unemployment and job vacancy rates returning to pre-pandemic levels. The growth rate reached about 3 percent in the last few years, and global output has approached its potential level. In this context, the global economy achieved a growth rate of 3.3 percent in 2024, compared to 3.2 percent in 2023. Global headline inflation also decreased during 2024, recording 5.8 percent, compared to 6.8 percent in 2023. This resilience in the global economy comes despite the wide divergence in cohesion across countries. While growth recorded a greater-than-expected slowdown in China, India, and the Euro area, in addition to a slight contraction in Japan, momentum in the United States remained strong, driven by consumption. As for emerging and developing economies, they have witnessed a slight decline in growth, affected by global uncertainty, trade tensions, and tightening financial conditions.

Global inflation remains on its downward path, despite signs of this decline slowing in some countries and its continued rise in a limited number of cases. Average headline inflation recorded 2.6% in advanced economies during 2024, while it reached 7.5% in emerging and developing economies. Global core inflation has stabilized at slightly over 2% during the last months of 2024. Indicators point to a slowdown in nominal wage growth, alongside signs of a gradual improvement in labor market conditions. Despite the decline of core inflation for goods to usual levels, services price inflation remains high compared to what it was before the COVID-19 pandemic, especially in the United States and the Euro area. Some emerging markets also continue

On the global monetary policy front, central banks continued to formulate their monetary policies during 2024 with a focus on domestic economic conditions and inflation, while taking into account counteracting external factors. In this context, central banks adopted a cautious monetary easing policy that in turn kept asset price volatility relatively low. The easing policy was reflected in the key interest rates of the Federal Reserve in the United States (4.6 points), the European Central Bank (3.15 points), and the Chinese Central Bank (1.5 points). It is noted that a few banks have started to raise interest rates. In this context, it was possible to contain the risks threatening financial stability in the short term. However, these easing financial conditions could facilitate the accumulation of vulnerabilities—such as overvaluation of assets, high levels of private and government debt, and the reliance of non-bank financial institutions on financial leverage—which will lead to increased economic uncertainty and uncertainty about future policies.

Global financial conditions are still characterized by a great deal of easing, despite the existence of divergences between different regions. Stock prices in advanced economies have risen, supported by expectations of more accommodative policies. In the United States, they recorded greater stimulus. In contrast, valuations in emerging and developing economies recorded lower levels. The tightening of monetary policy continued, and the US dollar continued to rise, driven by expectations of the imposition of new customs duties and rising interest rates.

The nominal value of total global debt rose during 2024 by about 7 trillion US dollars, to reach a new record level of 318 trillion US dollars by the end of the year. In parallel, the ratio of debt to global GDP rose to about 328 percent, the first increase in four years, with the slowdown in total GDP growth and the retreat of inflationary pressures. The debt of emerging markets reached a record 104 trillion US dollars at the end of 2024, with a ratio to GDP of 245 percent. Many emerging markets have witnessed a significant deterioration in their debt-bearing capacity in recent years, with the narrowing of the gap in growth rates between them and the advanced economies, and the continued rise in government interest expenses.

Regarding capital flows, emerging markets witnessed a notable increase in non-resident capital inflows, with net inflows into these markets exceeding 900 billion US dollars during 2024, an increase of about 32% compared to 2023. China captured a large share of this. These flows consist of portfolio investment flows worth 274 billion US dollars, and foreign direct investment flows estimated to rise to about 426 billion US dollars, driven by the recovery in major economies such as China, India, and Mexico, and other investment flows estimated at about 218 billion US dollars.

Priorities in economic, financial, and monetary policymaking are focused on carefully calibrating short-term policies to ensure a soft economic landing. Many countries need to adjust their public finance policy approach to help put public debt on a sustainable path and build appropriate fiscal safety buffers, in line with each country’s circumstances. Restructuring is essential to improve medium-term growth prospects, along with the need to continue supporting the most vulnerable groups and strengthening social safety nets. The success of these reforms also requires strategies that enhance their social acceptance, as this is a fundamental condition for their effective implementation. However, the success of all these efforts requires broader international cooperation, aimed in particular at accelerating the transition to a green economy, supporting debt restructuring efforts, and mitigating the risks of geo-economic fragmentation. It is crucial to strengthen rule-based multilateral frameworks to ensure all economies benefit from future growth opportunities.

At the local level, as a result of a number of internal and external accumulations caused by regional turmoil since 2011 on the one hand, particularly related to the repercussions of the Syrian crisis, its risks, and the burdens of displacement it caused; the repercussions of the Corona crisis on the other hand; the difficulties facing public finances in terms of budget deficit and the exacerbation of public debt and its service on a third hand; the Russian-Ukrainian crisis on a fourth hand; and the state of instability that prevailed in the region following the Israeli war on Gaza and Lebanon on a fifth hand; the Lebanese economy has faced, for the fifth consecutive year, pressing conditions as a result of the multidimensional crisis it is going through. This renewed crisis is represented by the local inflationary financial-monetary factor, the imbalance in the balance of payments and the lack of liquidity, the factor related to the local energy crisis of fuel and electricity and its general economic effects, and the factor linked to global disruptions in supply chains and imbalances in energy and food markets.

The recent Israeli war led to thousands of deaths and injuries, and the displacement of nearly 1.2 million people in Lebanon, which has had devastating effects on the lives of citizens and their social and economic conditions. The World Bank estimates the value of material damages at about 6.8 billion US dollars, and economic losses at about 7.2 billion US dollars, which exceeds half of Lebanon’s Gross Domestic Product. Lebanon continues to suffer from the burdens of the displacement crisis, as it is considered a center for the largest number of refugees/displaced persons per capita in the world.

This situation has put significant pressure on public services, infrastructure, the labor market, and the environment. The severity of the economic crisis was exacerbated by the Lebanese economy’s heavy reliance on imports, making it highly vulnerable to price shocks. The impact of this reality was reflected in a sharp decline in key economic indicators since late 2019, affecting the foreign trade, tourism, investment, consumption, and public spending sectors, coinciding with an unprecedented rise in prices. However, the last quarter of 2024 witnessed positive developments with the implementation of a ceasefire, political breakthroughs, and the emergence of signs of a return to the path of reform.

The macroeconomy in Lebanon recorded a contraction of 6.4 percent during 2024, according to Banque du Liban estimates. The annual inflation rate reached 45.2 percent, compared to 221.3 percent for the year 2023. Additionally, the balance of payments (change in the net foreign assets of the financial sector)¹ recorded a surplus of 6.44 billion US dollars during 2024, compared to a surplus of 2.2 billion US dollars during 2023.² The trade balance deficit reached about 14.2 billion US dollars in 2024, which is 16 percent (ratio of exports to imports), compared to about 14.5 billion US dollars in 2023, recording a decrease of 2.3 percent compared to a decrease of 6.6 percent in the previous year. This development resulted from a decrease in imports by 3.6 percent and a decrease in exports by 9.6 percent during 2024, reaching about 16.9 billion US dollars and about 2.7 billion US dollars, respectively. As for remittances from expatriates to Lebanon, they recorded 6.8 billion US dollars during 2024, according to Banque du Liban estimates.

On the monetary level, and as part of its efforts to unify the multiple exchange rates, Banque du Liban adopted a new official exchange rate of /89,500/ Lebanese pounds per US dollar starting from the first of January 2024. This new rate was applied to all banking transactions instead of the previously adopted rate of /1,500/ Lebanese pounds. The exchange rate of the Lebanese pound maintained its stability throughout the year, fluctuating slightly within the range of 89,600 – 89,700 Lebanese pounds per US dollar, even amid intensified pressures during the last quarter. This stability was achieved thanks to the implementation of strict traditional monetary policies by Banque du Liban, which included tight control over the money supply and a halt to government financing. Section Two: The Executive Framework for Monetary Policy - Objectives, Tools, and Mechanisms

Within the executive framework for monetary policy, Banque du Liban has worked to achieve a set of basic objectives, the most prominent of which are: mitigating the impact of successive shocks on individuals and institutions by maintaining the exchange rate of the lira against the US dollar at stable levels, and ensuring the stability of the banking and financial sectors at this stage, which is characterized by stagnation and a high degree of uncertainty, pending the launch of the reform and restructuring phase. In addition to that, the bank focused on improving payment and transfer systems, managing liquidity, and curbing inflation rates, as well as contributing to public debt management.

In this context, the banking sector continued to face significant challenges in capital and liquidity, which limited its ability to effectively support the economic recovery. This reality was reflected in a decline in several indicators, as total bank assets decreased by 10 percent during the year to reach /9,197,061/ billion Lebanese Lira at the end of 2024 (equivalent to about 102.7 billion US dollars).⁴ Also, deposits of the resident and non-resident private sector with the banking sector contracted by 6 percent annually, compared to a 24.5 percent decline in 2023, to reach about /8,537,911/ billion Lebanese Lira (equivalent to about 88.4 billion US dollars) at the end of 2024. In parallel, the lending activity to the private sector recorded an annual decline of about 32 percent in 2024 compared to 59.2 percent in 2023, with loans granted to the (non-financial) private sector reaching about /494,701/ billion Lebanese Lira (equivalent to about 5.5 billion US dollars) at the end of 2024, compared to 2.3 billion US dollars to the public sector. Loans to the private sector constituted 6.3 percent of total deposits at the end of December 2024, compared to 8.3 percent at the end of December 2023. Likewise, the dollarization rate of deposits (at the banking sector level) rose to 99.1 percent at the end of 2024, compared to 95.8 percent at the end of 2023, while the dollarization rate of lending increased to 97.7 percent at the end of 2024, compared to 90.88 percent at the end of 2023. The number of branches of Lebanese banks abroad reached 44 branches. The aggregate capital base of commercial banks reached about 4.8 billion US dollars at the end of 2024, representing a decline of 5.3 percent compared to the end of 2023.

The resumption of the banking sector’s effective role depends on finding comprehensive legal solutions. This objective can only be achieved by providing an integrated environment that includes: enacting reform legislation, restructuring public finances, addressing the issue of depositors’ funds, and restoring confidence in the banking sector, in addition to restoring the flow of liquidity to banks. This context would enable Banque du Liban, in its capacity as the regulator, to take stricter measures to reduce reliance on the cash economy and enhance the role of banks in the economic cycle.

Despite Banque du Liban taking several regulatory measures during 2024 to strengthen the framework for combating money laundering and the financing of terrorism in Lebanon, as part of Lebanon’s broader efforts to address strategic deficiencies in line with its commitments within the work of the Financial Action Task Force (FATF), Lebanon was not able to implement 21 basic recommendations before the grace period expired, while another 25 recommendations were only partially implemented. As a result, the Financial Action Task Force (FATF) placed Lebanon on the “list of countries subject to increased monitoring – the grey list” on October 25, 2024.

Regarding the traditional mechanisms followed by Banque du Liban to implement its monetary policy, the following tools were used during the year 2024:

  • Maintaining monetary stability. Events and developments continued during 2024 as a result of the ongoing war in Gaza, casting its weight and repercussions on the entire region and on Lebanon. The intermittent security tensions had a direct impact on the economic, financial, and banking conditions, as well as on tourism and cash flows from abroad. The situation deteriorated dramatically when the war began in September in Lebanon. Despite this difficult and exceptional situation, Banque du Liban continued to establish monetary and financial stability, in coordination and cooperation with the Ministry of Finance, with the aim of not increasing the money supply, which it successfully maintained between 52 and 60 trillion Lebanese Lira.⁵ It also secured the stability of the US dollar exchange rate at an average of 89,500 Lebanese Lira throughout the year. With the aim of providing relief to depositors, Banque du Liban disbursed additional months through Circulars 158 and 166, which assisted the beneficiaries and did not affect the central bank’s liquid assets. The rise in gold prices had a significant impact on the central bank’s gold reserves; after the price of an ounce was 2,063 US dollars at the end of 2023, it recorded 2,624 US dollars at the end of 2024, which raised the value of the gold reserve by more than five billion US dollars during 2024 alone.
  • Ensuring the relative stability of interest rates in the national currency and foreign currencies within acceptable and stable margins, without limiting the freedom of financial markets and dealers, within the framework of applicable laws and regulations. In parallel, both the lending and deposit interest rates (interest on deposits) in Lebanese Lira increased by 164 basis points and 303 basis points, respectively. As for the interest rate on US dollar accounts, the lending rate increased by 175 basis points and stabilized on the deposit side. As a result, the spread between the lending rates in Lira and in dollars narrowed from 202 points in December 2023 to 191 points in December 2024, while the spread between the savings rates in Lira and in dollars increased from 52 points in December 2023 to 355 points in December 2024. In addition, no treasury bills were issued during 2024 based on a decision from the Ministry of Finance.
  • Striving to enhance confidence in the national currency by controlling the money supply in Lebanese Lira. The currency in circulation in Lebanese Lira increased by 12.9 percent at the end of 2024 compared to a decrease of 27 percent at the end of 2023, reaching approximately /65,564/ billion Lebanese Lira at the end of 2024.
